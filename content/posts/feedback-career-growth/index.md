Feedback can be scary—it can be a critique from a reader, a complaint from a customer, or even a rejection of a dream job. When I was a child and young student, I felt intimidated by comments from others. Directly facing how other people view you (or your work) takes courage; the skill of giving and taking feedback doesn't come naturally. But when feedback is delivered and received intentionally, it can serve as a powerful tool for your goals. 

In this article, I will focus on good feedback practices in a professional setting, though I have found them quite valuable in my personal life as well – effectively communicating one's needs and feelings is a critical part of any human relationship. 

## Why feedback is powerful and necessary?

When we pursue a goal or advance our career, we need guidance along the way. Nothing feels worse than working hard for your dream but realizing you're moving in the wrong direction, farther away from your end goal. 

In some worst-case scenarios, I've seen team members get fired and feel completely confused, since from their perspective they had not received any warning signs that they could work on or prepare for. If not managed properly, such cases can significantly hurt team morale and challenge leaders' credibilities.   
	  
In professional settings, both the giver and recipient of feedback need to make efforts to cultivate an open and positive culture. 

## What makes up good feedback? (concrete + actionable)

Similar to Tolstoy's famous quote in Anna Karenina: **All good feedback is alike; bad feedback is bad in its own way.**

**A piece of good feedback must have a concrete observation and an actionable plan to back it.** In principle, feedback should focus on the recipient's behavior, the impact of the behavior, and an action plan. Importantly, when you give comments on someone's work, there are usually positive comments too; positive comments give the recipient a morale boost and direction as well. Delivering positive and constructive feedback in one conversation is usually more effective – psychologically, the recipient would not feel attacked and tends to be more accepting of the feedback.  

Here are some examples of good practices:  
 

1. Code review:   
   1. **Feedback**: John, you can improve on making your code cleaner and production ready. If I approve your recent PRs without request for editing, the changes in the PRs would make our codebase hard to maintain in the future.   
   2. **Example**: go through a few PRs where similar code blocks could be abstracted as a helper function with neat doctoring  
   3. **Action**: John's manager will share some good code examples in their production system which John will review and learn from the pattern.   
2. Client communication:    
   1. **Feedback**: Jane, when you email clients, your communication should be more organized and easy for clients to digest. When I read your email to Jack (a senior client) today, I couldn't grasp the main takeaways of the email within 60 seconds.  
   2. **Example**: Jane's manager walks her through a recent email from Jane to Jack, where she conveyed her message by three mid-size paragraphs without calling out where the client should pay attention to.  
   3. **Action**: Jane's manager will forward a few best practices email examples so that Jane can imitate the style.

## Feedback cadence: formal 1:1s and adhoc discussion

When I started at BCG as a young professional, I was taught how  to give and receive feedback. BCG's feedback training is quite comprehensive, which might seem too intense for people in other  industries. Therefore, I would summarize the training with two principal ideas: **set up a routine performance discussion; allow flexible and ad hoc feedback.** Readers can apply the two principles based on their company's culture and dynamics.  

The following is an example of general good practice between manager and team member at BCG:

1. **Formal performance discussion:** The manager and team members set up recurrent 20-30 minute formal 1:1 meetings every two weeks to discuss feedback to each other. They should avoid discussing specific work related content in the meeting; the focus of the meeting should be only about performance and career advice.   
     
   **Example**: When a data scientist manager and a data scientist have their bi-weekly 1:1 meeting, they should discuss the quality of the junior member's code and how they should improve, but should not discuss functionalities of the junior member's code.    
     
2. **Informal feedback:** This can happen when manager or team members observe some feedback opportunities at work and think it's worth bringing it up on the spot. Informal feedback occurs between formal feedback meetings, which complements the latter since people have fresher memories about the event when feedback is given shortly after.   
     
   **Example**: A manager can set aside five minutes to provide feedback after a team member presents in a client meeting. 

## Feedback conversation should be a dialogue

In an ideal world, all the good practices discussed above should be a dialogue: managers give feedback to team members, and vice versa. However, let's be honest, it can be hard for team members to give honest and constructive feedback to managers. I'd advise junior team members to build friendly, trusting relationships with their manager and follow good feedback practices when giving constructive feedback. Unfortunately, jerk managers exist, but you can always choose to prioritize yourself and walk away from a toxic environment.

For managers, it's an art to manage up and down to keep everybody including customers and clients happy. Your life will be generally better when your team members are growing in their careers and being productive. Taking their feedback, even when it's constructive, can help you master the art of management.
