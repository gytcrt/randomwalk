I’m in Paris this week, which is about 2 times as large as Manhattan and home to 2 million Parisians. Compared to NYC, Paris has no lack of mind-blowing restaurants and museums, though I found AI’s footprint to be surprisingly lighter here. This observation got me thinking and jotting down the following notes during my journey. 

## Google doesn’t automatically return AI summarized result at the top

Google has been showing an AI summarized results at the top of a search result for a while, but I have heard many complaints about this feature from both my non-tech peers and fellow technical bloggers. These people are not AI haters, but instead want the autonomy to choose when and how AI content is presented to them.   

However, during my time in Paris, I realized Google does not always force this AI feature on users here. Below are two screenshots of searches on my laptop and phone. Google returned some summary results with reference links, like the old days. I love it. I can choose to click on the link and verify the number myself or scroll down to find my preferred sources.

After doing some quick research online, it seems that the lack of AI search results is due to {{< newtabref href="https://www.reddit.com/r/google/comments/1peau6g/just_a_reminder_that_ai_mode_in_search_in/" title="regulatory challenges in France" >}}. The EU is currently {{< newtabref href="https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/crl95eg33k1o" title="investigating Google over AI-generated summaries in search results" >}}, because Google failed to offer compensation to content creators behind the AI summarized answer. Needless to say, legislation around AI development and usage ought to catch up across the world. 

## No weird AI ads in subway or on the street of Paris

If you live in NYC and have not noticed some bizarre AI advertisements in the subway or on the streets of Manhattan, I envy you. The following ad on the F train has stirred some negative conversations about AI and technology within my “human” friend group. We don’t remotely think AI is a viable solution to modern alienation. If anything, AI might have deepened the epidemic of loneliness in the US. Similar AI ads are everywhere in NYC, but I’d rather not post them in my blog and accidentally promote them. 

{{< figurelightbox src="ai_ads.jpg" caption="Weird AI ads in NYC subway F train" alt="AI companion advertisement on a NYC subway F train" align="center" width="80%" >}}

Many of us moved to NYC for its rich and diverse culture. Even though I work in AI and believe in its value, as a New Yorker, I don’t want to be treated as an empty vessel whose role is to help the AI sector in the US grow. 

Weird AI or robotic ads are not only pervasive, but also promoted as a {{< newtabref href="https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/17/humanoid-robots-perform-advanced-martial-arts-at-chinese-new-year-gala" title="mainstream national priority in China" >}}. During my time in France,I heard one joke (probably adapted from Emily in Paris) from the Chinese internet: French work to live, Americans live to work, and Chinese are born to work. 

## My takeaways
Paris is a mirror to NYC, and history presents a lesson to current innovators and politicians. 

As I revisited histories of the industrial revolution lately, I was reminded that revolutionary technologies tend to disrupt existing social structure. If AI is kicking off the fourth industrial revolution, I hope we can collectively learn from history and minimize destructive impact to the most vulnerable populations in our societies.
